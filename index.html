What I learned from this book Radical Acceptance

❑ First off, be kinder to yourself. Seriously, we are often our harshest critics. You don't have to beat yourself up every time you make a mistake. Instead, practice self-compassion. Treat yourself the way you would treat a good friend who's having a hard time. Say encouraging things to yourself instead of focusing on the negative. It makes a huge difference.
❑Another thing is to practice mindfulness. This doesn't mean you have to meditate for hours or anything. It's more about paying attention to your thoughts and feelings without judging them. Just let them be. It's kind of like observing your thoughts as they come and go without getting too caught up in them. When you can do that, you start to realize that you don't have to believe every negative thought that pops into your head.

What I learned from this book Radical Acceptance

❑Then there's shame and guilt. These are heavy feelings, and they can keep you stuck if you let them. If you feel guilty about something, acknowledge it, make amends if you can, but then let it go. Shame is trickier, but it's often about feeling like you're fundamentally flawed. Tara Brach suggests that with mindfulness and compassion, you can start to heal from these feelings. It's not an overnight thing, but with practice, you can lighten that load.
❑Finally, remember that nobody's perfect. I know, it sounds cliché, but it's true. We all have our flaws and shortcomings, and that's okay. Embrace imperfection. When you stop trying to be perfect, you open yourself up to a more authentic and fulfilling life. It's a relief to just be who you are, without the constant pressure to measure up to some unrealistic standard.
